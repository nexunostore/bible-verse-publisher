Mark Chapter 3 (Notes)
categories: The Gospel, New Testament, Bible Study


**Who They Are**

Mark 3 is less about a single person and more about Jesus at a turning point in His ministry, surrounded by several key groups:

- **Jesus** – Presented as the authoritative Son of God whose ministry is expanding in power and opposition.
- **The Disciples / The Twelve** – Chosen and appointed as apostles, sharing in His mission to preach and cast out demons.
- **Religious Leaders** – Pharisees and scribes who increasingly oppose Him and eventually plot His death.
- **The Crowds** – Huge multitudes drawn by His healings and exorcisms, but often misunderstanding who He is.
- **Jesus’ Family** – Those who think He is “out of His mind,” highlighting tension between natural family and spiritual family.

Mark 3 weaves these figures together to show rising conflict, the formation of a new community (the Twelve and the “whoever does God’s will” family), and Jesus’ unmistakable authority over 
sickness, demons, and even traditional religious boundaries.

 **Life Story / Key Events**

Drawing especially on the socio-rhetorical commentary excerpt, Mark 3 sits at a hinge point in the Gospel. Chapter 3 itself contains:

1. **Healing on the Sabbath and Conflict with Pharisees (3:1–6)**  
   - Jesus heals a man with a withered hand in the synagogue on the Sabbath.  
   - This act intensifies conflict: the Pharisees and Herodians begin to **plot to destroy Him**, marking a major escalation.

2. **Massive Crowds and Summary of His Ministry (3:7–12)**  
   - Jesus withdraws to the sea; great crowds from Galilee, Judea, Jerusalem, Idumea, beyond the Jordan, Tyre, and Sidon come to Him.
   - He heals many; people press in just to touch Him.
   - **Unclean spirits** fall before Him, crying out that He is the **Son of God**.  
   - As the Witherington commentary notes, this passage “brings together Mark’s portrait of Jesus’ ministry from 1:16 to 3:6…with a clear focus on Jesus being the Son of God,” and serves as a 
**transition** into the coming miracle cycle (4:35–6:56).

3. **Appointment of the Twelve Apostles (3:13–19)**  
   - Jesus goes up on a mountain and **calls those He wants**.
   - He appoints **twelve** “that they might be with Him and that He might send them out to preach and to have authority to cast out demons.”
   - Their names are listed, emphasizing Jesus’ intentional formation of a new leadership core.

4. **Accusation: “He Has Beelzebul” (3:20–30)**  
   - Crowds press in again; Jesus and His disciples can’t even eat.
   - Scribes from Jerusalem say He’s possessed by Beelzebul and drives out demons by the ruler of demons.
   - Jesus answers with parables about a kingdom divided against itself and the binding of the strong man, asserting that His exorcisms show **God’s kingdom conquering Satan**, not collusion 
with him.
   - He warns about **blasphemy against the Holy Spirit** (attributing God’s work to Satan).

5. **Redefining Family (3:31–35)**  
   - Jesus’ mother and brothers arrive and call for Him.
   - Jesus asks, “Who is my mother, or my brothers?” and points to those seated around Him, saying, “Whoever does the will of God is my brother and sister and mother.”
   - This re-centers identity around **obedience to God’s will**, not bloodline.


 **Character & Growth**

Since Mark 3 is a snapshot rather than a long arc, we see more of Jesus’ established character and the development of others’ responses to Him than “growth” in the usual sense.

 Jesus

- **Authoritative and Fearless**  
  He heals boldly on the Sabbath despite knowing it will provoke hostility. He confronts religious accusations directly, using parables about divided kingdoms and the strong man.
- **Compassionate but Not Crowd-Driven**  
  He heals many and engages with the crowds, yet He also withdraws and deliberately selects the Twelve rather than being ruled by popularity.
- **Self-understanding as Divine Son**  
  The commentary highlights that Mark presents Him as the **divine Son of God**: even demons recognize Him, and subsequent stories (calming the storm, walking on water) will show Him as an 
“epiphany of God himself.”

 The Disciples (The Twelve)

- They are **called, not self-selected**; chosen “that they might be with Him” and then sent out.
- They are given **authority to preach and to cast out demons**, showing Jesus sharing His mission.
- Mark’s broader narrative (not fully in the excerpt) suggests they will often misunderstand Him; in this early step, though, they are portrayed predominantly as **learners and emissaries**.

 Religious Leaders and Crowds

- **Religious Leaders** move from questioning to **active plotting** His destruction, and from skepticism to **slander** (“He has Beelzebul”), showing hardening hearts.
- **Crowds** recognize His power but largely **miss His identity**. As Witherington notes, “The supernatural world knows well who Jesus is…but mere mortals still do not really know who Jesus 
is even though he has performed great miracles.”

 Jesus’ Family

- They appear concerned or skeptical (Mark 3 implies they think He’s “out of His mind” and come to restrain Him).
- The text doesn’t show them embracing His mission here; instead, their tension with Him highlights the cost of His calling.

 The “Messianic Secret” and Demons’ Recognition

- Demons recognize Him as the Son of God, while human onlookers do not.  
- This fits a recurring Markan pattern: **spiritual beings know who He is, humans struggle**.  
- Theologically, some see this as:
  - A way Mark shows the depth of human spiritual blindness.
  - A narrative device delaying full understanding until the cross and resurrection.

 Conflict and Kingdom

- The **Sabbath healing** and **Beelzebul controversy** frame Jesus’ ministry as a clash of kingdoms:
  - His exorcisms are signs of **God’s kingdom overthrowing Satan**, “binding the strong man.”
  - Religious opposition shows that God’s kingdom **confronts existing religious structures** when they oppose mercy and truth.


 Redefining Family and Community

- Jesus’ final words in the chapter redefine family around **doing God’s will**.
- This anticipates the early church’s identity as a new family in Christ, transcending ethnic and bloodline boundaries.
