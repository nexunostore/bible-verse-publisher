Luke Chapter 1 (Notes)
categories: The Gospel, New Testament, Bible Study


 **Luke Chapter 1: The Beginning of the Greatest Story Ever Told**

Luke begins his Gospel in a way unlike any other Gospel writer. Before the angels appear, before the shepherds arrive, before Jesus is born in Bethlehem, Luke pauses to explain
why he is writing.

He addresses a man named Theophilus and explains that he has carefully investigated the events surrounding Jesus so that his readers may have confidence in what they have been
taught. Luke is not presenting a collection of disconnected stories. He is opening a carefully arranged account of God's work in history—a story that begins with promises made
long before and moves toward the arrival of the Messiah.

Luke chapter 1 is the doorway into this story. It introduces two miraculous births, two faithful women, two prophetic songs, and a message that the promises of God are finally
coming to fulfillment.

 **Two Announcements, Two Miraculous Births**

The chapter begins in Jerusalem, inside the Temple, where a priest named Zechariah is serving before God. He and his wife Elizabeth are described as righteous people who 
faithfully followed God's commands, yet they carried a deep sorrow: they had no child, and they were both advanced in years.

During Zechariah's priestly service, the angel Gabriel appears and announces that Elizabeth will have a son. This child will be John the Baptist, the one who will prepare the 
way for the Lord.

The announcement echoes many moments in Israel's history when God brought life from impossible circumstances. Like Sarah, Hannah, and other women before her, Elizabeth would 
experience the power of God overcoming human limitations.

Yet Zechariah struggles to believe the message. His question reveals the tension between human uncertainty and divine promise: how can this happen when circumstances seem 
impossible? As a sign, Gabriel tells him that he will be unable to speak until the promise is fulfilled.

The silence that follows becomes a period of waiting—a reminder that sometimes God works even when He appears quiet.

Six months later, Gabriel appears again. This time he does not come to Jerusalem's Temple but to Nazareth, a small and seemingly insignificant village in Galilee. There he 
visits a young woman named Mary.

The contrast could hardly be greater.

Zechariah was an elderly priest serving in the center of Israel's religious life. Mary was a young woman from a humble village with no public status or influence. Yet it is to 
Mary that God reveals the greatest announcement in history.

She will conceive a son and name Him Jesus. He will be called the Son of the Most High, and God will give Him the throne of David forever.

The promise reaches back to God's covenant with David, where God promised that David's kingdom would have an enduring future. The child Mary would bear was not simply another 
king—He was the fulfillment of Israel's hope.

Mary's response becomes one of the most memorable expressions of faith in Scripture:

*"Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word."*

Her response is not based on having every question answered. It is based on trusting the One who gave the promise.

 **The Meeting of Two Mothers**

After Gabriel's announcement, Mary travels to visit Elizabeth. When she arrives, something extraordinary happens: the unborn John leaps in Elizabeth's womb, and Elizabeth is 
filled with the Holy Spirit.

Before John ever begins his ministry, he is already pointing toward Jesus.

Elizabeth recognizes the significance of Mary's visit and declares that Mary is blessed because she believed that God's promise would be fulfilled.

Mary then responds with a song known as the Magnificat.

This song reveals Mary's understanding of what God is doing. She does not see herself as important because of her position or status. Instead, she rejoices because God has 
looked upon her humble condition and chosen to work through her.

The Magnificat celebrates a recurring theme throughout Scripture: God lifts the humble, remembers the forgotten, and overturns human ideas of greatness.

The proud are brought down. The hungry are filled. The promises made to Abraham and his descendants are being remembered.

Mary's song echoes the prayers of faithful people before her, especially Hannah's prayer in 1 Samuel 2. Like Hannah, Mary recognizes that God is able to transform situations 
that appear hopeless.

But Mary's song also looks forward. The themes she celebrates—the reversal of worldly values, God's mercy toward the humble, and His kingdom breaking into the world—will 
become central themes throughout Jesus' ministry.

 **Zechariah's Song: God Has Remembered His Promise**

When John is born, another moment of praise takes place.

According to Jewish custom, the child is brought for circumcision and naming. Everyone expects him to be called Zechariah after his father, but Elizabeth insists that his name
will be John, just as the angel commanded.

When Zechariah confirms this in writing, his ability to speak returns.

His first words are not words of complaint or explanation. They are words of worship.

His song, known as the Benedictus, celebrates God's faithfulness. God has not forgotten His people. He has remembered His covenant and raised up salvation through the house of
David.

Zechariah sees John's role clearly: he will prepare the way for the coming Lord by calling people to repentance and pointing them toward salvation.

The chapter closes with a simple statement about John's growth:

*"The child grew and became strong in spirit."*

The stage has been set. The prophet who will prepare the way has arrived. The Savior who will fulfill God's promises is coming.

 **The God Who Works Through the Unexpected**

One of the strongest themes in Luke chapter 1 is that God often chooses unexpected people and unexpected places.

The announcement of John's birth comes in the Temple, but the announcement of Jesus' birth comes in a small village.

A priest receives a promise, but struggles with doubt. A young woman receives an impossible calling, but responds with faith.

God's kingdom does not begin through political power, wealth, or human achievement. It begins through obedience, humility, and trust.

Luke's opening chapter reminds readers that God's plans are often larger than what people can immediately see. The elderly couple who had lost hope would become part of God's 
preparation. The young woman from Nazareth would become the mother of the Messiah. The child who seemed ordinary would prepare the world for the arrival of the King.

 **Lessons from Luke Chapter 1**

 *Faith does not require understanding everything*

Mary asks how God's promise will happen, but her question is different from Zechariah's. She seeks understanding, not proof. Her faith rests on God's character rather than her
circumstances.

 *God remembers His promises*

Throughout the chapter, the language of promise appears again and again. The births of John and Jesus are not random events. They are part of a story God began generations 
earlier.

 *God sees the humble*

Mary's song reminds us that God is not limited by human measures of importance. The world may overlook ordinary people, but God sees and uses those who trust Him.

 *Waiting can be part of God's work*

Zechariah's silence was not meaningless. It became a season of reflection before praise. God's timing often involves preparation before fulfillment.

 **The Beginning of the Gospel Story**

Luke chapter 1 is more than an introduction to the birth of Jesus. It is a declaration that God is faithful.

The promises spoken through prophets, the hopes carried by generations, and the prayers of faithful people are all moving toward one moment: the arrival of Jesus Christ.

The chapter begins with an elderly priest unable to speak and ends with a young child growing in preparation for his mission. Between those moments, heaven announces that 
God's greatest promise has arrived.

The Savior is coming.
