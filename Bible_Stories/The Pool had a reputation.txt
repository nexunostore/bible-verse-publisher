The pool with a reputation.  
categories: Miracles,

The pool had a reputation.  **Bethesda**

Tucked just inside Jerusalem’s Sheep Gate, Bethesda—“house of mercy” or “house of grace”—was where desperate people gathered. They came with broken bodies and thin hopes, crowding under five 
stone porches that promised shade, if not healing. Some believed that from time to time the waters were stirred by an angel and that the first one in would be healed (John 5:3–4; 5:7). 
Whether that belief was fact or legend, the result was the same: a mass of suffering people, all waiting, all watching the water, all hoping to be first.

Into that scene walked Jesus.

John 5:1–15 gives us the story of one man among that multitude—a paralytic who had been sick for thirty‑eight years—and the Messiah who met him. Around that meeting John weaves themes of 
grace, faith, Sabbath, and the true nature of healing. For us, the story offers a mirror and an invitation.

Let’s walk through it.

---

## 1. The Setting: A Real Place, Filled with Real Pain

John is careful with details:

> “Now there is in Jerusalem by the Sheep Gate a pool, in Aramaic called Bethesda, which has five roofed colonnades. In these lay a multitude of invalids—blind, lame, and paralyzed” (John 
5:2–3).

For centuries some skeptics dismissed this as a pious fiction; no such pool, they said, could be identified. That changed in the late nineteenth century. Excavations just north of the Temple 
Mount, beside St. Anne’s Church, uncovered a two‑basin pool complex with:

- Two adjacent pools divided by a central wall  
- Remains of columns and porticoes (porches) around and between the pools  
- A narrow stairway leading down into the water  

In other words, exactly what John described: a retaining pool with five covered colonnades, accessible by steps, matching the outlines given in John 5. Archaeologists have even suggested that 
the pool may be the same as the “Upper Pool” mentioned in 2 Kings 18:17 and Isaiah 7:3; 36:2, a long‑standing water system later adapted for ritual use.

So we’re not in the realm of myth. We’re standing in a real place, among real ruins, where real people once waited. Bethesda is not just a theological symbol; it’s a concrete reminder that 
the Gospel unfolds in history.

And that history is soaked in human pain:

> “In these lay a multitude of invalids—blind, lame, and paralyzed.”

Bethesda was the ancient world’s waiting room for the chronically ill—people worn down not just by sickness, but by disappointment. Perhaps you know that feeling: countless attempts, no real 
change; watching others “step in” before you; the growing suspicion that maybe your turn will never come.

Into that discouragement, Jesus comes.

---

## 2. The Question That Exposes the Heart: “Do You Want to Be Healed?”

John narrows the camera from the multitude to one man:

> “One man was there who had been an invalid for thirty‑eight years. When Jesus saw him lying there and knew that he had already been there a long time, he said to him, ‘Do you want to be 
healed?’” (John 5:5–6).

It is a startling question. Of course he wants to be healed—or does he?

Jesus’ question is not cruel; it is probing. He is not merely asking about physical desire; he is pressing on the man’s whole posture toward his condition.

The man’s reply is revealing:

> “Sir, I have no one to put me into the pool when the water is stirred up, and while I am going another steps down before me” (5:7).

Notice what he doesn’t say: “Yes.”  
Instead, he explains why he hasn’t been healed:

- “I have no one.”  
- “Others get there first.”  
- “The problem is the system; the problem is other people.”

In other words, his hope is entirely tied to the pool and to a very specific method of healing. His imagination is captive to a formula: if the water moves, and if I can just be first, then I 
might be made well.

We often stand in that same place. We are convinced we know exactly how God must work if we are to be helped:

- “If I can just get that job...”  
- “If God will heal *this* condition in *this* way...”  
- “If this person changes, then I’ll be OK.”

Jesus’ question cuts through that: “Do you actually want to be healed, or are you primarily committed to the way you think healing must come?”

It’s one thing to want relief; it’s another to be ready for the change real healing brings.

---

## 3. The Word That Creates What It Commands

Jesus doesn’t debate the pool. He doesn’t correct the man’s theology about angels and stirring waters. He simply speaks:

> “Jesus said to him, ‘Get up, take up your bed, and walk.’ And at once the man was healed, and he took up his bed and walked” (5:8–9).

Three quick observations.

### 3.1. Healing by the Word, Not by the Water

The man is healed not by entering the pool but by hearing and obeying the word of Christ. His long‑held hope that “someday, if I get into the right water at the right moment, I might be 
healed” is quietly overturned. Healing is not in the pool; it is in the person standing before him.

The Bible is full of unexpected means of healing—a pot of stew purified (2 Kings 4), washing in the Jordan (2 Kings 5), the shadow of Peter, Paul’s handkerchiefs. God is creative. But the 
decisive point here is that Jesus himself is the source. The Pool of Bethesda becomes the backdrop for a deeper truth: mercy isn’t ultimately in a place or a ritual, but in the Messiah.

### 3.2. A Command That Enables

“Get up, take up your bed, and walk” would be cruel words—if they came from anyone else. The paralyzed man had no strength to obey such a command. But the moment Jesus speaks, the command 
carries its own power. The word that orders is also the word that enables.

That is a pattern of the Gospel. When Christ commands the spiritually dead to live, to repent, to follow, his word is not mere instruction; it is re‑creation. Like the breath of God animating 
Adam (Gen. 2:7), Jesus’ voice brings life where there was none.

### 3.3. Instantaneous Change, Ongoing Fragility

“And at once the man was healed” (5:9). Thirty‑eight years of paralysis vanish in an instant. Yet, as Augustine reminded his congregation, even the limbs Jesus strengthened eventually 
weakened again in death. The blind eyes he opened eventually closed.

Physical miracles in the Gospels are real, but they are also signs—pointers to a final healing in the resurrection, when what is made new will never grow old, and what is healed will never 
again be sick. If we stop at the temporal miracle, we miss the deeper mercy.

---

## 4. The Collision with Religion: Healing on the Sabbath

John now drops a detail that ignites the controversy:

> “Now that day was the Sabbath” (5:9).

The religious leaders see the healed man carrying his bedroll through the city:

> “So the Jews said to the man who had been healed, ‘It is the Sabbath, and it is not lawful for you to take up your bed’” (5:10).

Technically, the Old Testament law did not forbid carrying a light mat on the Sabbath. The fourth commandment called God’s people to rest from ordinary work, to cease from their labors as God 
did from His (Ex. 20:8–11). But in the centuries that followed, a dense hedge of additional rules grew up around that command—dozens upon dozens of meticulous prohibitions to ensure no one 
came close to working. Carrying a mat got classified as “work.”

Jesus repeatedly collided with these traditions throughout the Gospels: when his disciples plucked grain on the Sabbath, when he healed a withered hand in the synagogue, when he opened the 
eyes of the blind on the holy day. Each time, the issue was the same: Who has authority to interpret the Sabbath, and what is its God‑given purpose?

In those other Sabbath controversies, Jesus framed the choice with piercing simplicity:

> “Is it lawful on the Sabbath to do good or to do harm, to save life or to destroy it?” (Luke 6:9).

If it’s permitted to rescue an animal from a pit on the Sabbath, how much more to restore a human being made in God’s image? The Sabbath was never meant to be an obstacle to mercy. As Jesus 
said elsewhere, “The Son of Man is lord of the Sabbath” (Luke 6:5). He doesn’t abolish the Sabbath; he restores it to its true center: doing good, giving life, revealing God’s heart.

At Bethesda, the religious leaders saw a man walking after thirty‑eight years of paralysis—and what gripped their attention was the bed in his hands, not the strength in his legs. Ritual had 
eclipsed love. Their obsession with regulations had blinded them to the very presence of the God who gave the law.

That contrast should unsettle us. It is entirely possible to be zealous for religious correctness and yet unmoved by grace standing in front of us. When our doctrines or traditions become 
excuses not to show mercy, we stand dangerously close to the position of the critics in this story.

---

## 5. From Physical to Spiritual: “Sin No More”

Later, Jesus finds the man in the temple and speaks again:

> “See, you are well! Sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you” (John 5:14).

Those words raise questions. Is Jesus saying that this man’s paralysis was caused by a specific sin? The text doesn’t spell that out. Elsewhere, Jesus explicitly rejects the idea that every 
illness is a direct judgment for personal sin (John 9:1–3). But here he draws a clear connection between sin and something “worse” than thirty‑eight years of disability.

Whatever exactly happened in this man’s past, Jesus shifts the focus from his body to his soul. Physical healing is a gift; spiritual restoration is critical. The “worse” thing is not another 
disease—it is final judgment, separation from God.

This is where Augustine’s insight is especially helpful: the truly lasting healing is what happens in the soul that believes. Bodies eventually die; forgiven, reborn souls live forever. The 
paralysis that should most terrify us is not in our legs but in our hearts—our inability to love God, to obey Him, to respond to His call apart from His grace.

Jesus did not come only to improve our circumstances; he came to forgive sin and give new life. The miracle at Bethesda is proof that he can do the deeper work. If he can restore wasted limbs 
with a word, he can certainly restore a wasted life.

---

## 6. The Witness of the Works: Healing as Testimony

The healing at Bethesda is not an isolated act of kindness; John places it at the front end of a long discourse where Jesus explains who he is. A few verses later, Jesus says:

> “The works that the Father has given me to accomplish, the very works that I am doing, bear witness about me that the Father has sent me” (5:36).

In other words, this miracle is not just about the man; it’s a sign about Jesus. The cripple walking through Jerusalem with his mat under his arm is a living sermon announcing:

- The Father has sent the Son.  
- The Son shares the Father’s authority, even over the Sabbath.  
- The Son gives life—as the Father gives life.  

Bethesda is a house of mercy because Mercy himself has walked in.

The tragedy is that many who knew the Scriptures best missed the One to whom the Scriptures pointed. They poured over the text but refused to come to Christ for life (John 5:39–40). They 
loved human approval more than the glory that comes from God (5:44). In the end, the paralytic believed enough to stand and walk; the religious experts remained stuck.

---

## 7. What the Pool of Bethesda Says to Us Today

This ancient story presses several questions into our lives.

### 7.1. Where Have You Put Your Hope?

Many of us have our own “pools of Bethesda”—the place, the method, the formula we are sure will fix us:

- “When I finally get married...”  
- “If I find the right therapist / church / program...”  
- “If God just answers *this* prayer in *that* way…”

God can use many means, but none of them are saviors. The paralytic’s hope was bound to the water; Jesus walked in and bypassed the system altogether.

Is there a place in your life where Jesus might be asking, “Do you want to be healed?”—which really means, “Will you trust *Me* more than your chosen method?”

### 7.2. Are You Willing for the Kind of Healing Jesus Brings?

Physical relief is precious, and Scripture encourages us to pray for it. But Jesus’ interaction with the healed man in the temple reminds us: he aims deeper than comfort. He calls for 
repentance: “Sin no more.”

Real healing often disrupts the patterns we’ve gotten used to. The man who carried his mat away from Bethesda now had a new responsibility to walk in obedience. In our lives, Jesus’ mercy 
will lead us not only out of suffering, but out of sin. Are we open to that?

### 7.3. Has Religion Made You Forget Mercy?

The Sabbath controversies in the Gospels expose a warning for every religious generation. It is possible to defend God’s law in ways that violate God’s heart.

Do people experience the “house of grace” when they are around us—especially the weak, the broken, the long‑discouraged? Or do they mostly encounter watchfulness, suspicion, and 
rule‑enforcement?

Jesus did not ignore the Sabbath; he fulfilled it by bringing rest, restoration, and joy. If he is Lord of our lives, our practice of faith must likewise be marked by mercy.

---

## Conclusion: The True House of Mercy

The ruins of Bethesda still sit in Jerusalem today—stone steps leading down into quiet, empty pools. The crowds are gone. The water no longer stirs.

But the mercy that walked there is alive.

The story of the paralytic at Bethesda is not merely a reminder that Jesus once healed a man long ago. It is an invitation to bring our own thirty‑eight‑year problems—our long disappointments, 
old sins, and chronic sorrows—to the One whose word still creates what it commands.

We do not have to wait by the water, straining to be first, hoping the moment is right. In Christ, the house of mercy has come to us.

And his question still stands:  
“Do you want to be healed?”
