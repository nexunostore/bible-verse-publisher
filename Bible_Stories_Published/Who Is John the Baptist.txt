John the Baptist
categories: Prophet, Forerunner, Baptizer
 
  **Who He Was**

John the Baptist was the forerunner of Jesus—a prophet, moral reformer, and preacher of messianic hope who called Israel to repentance shortly before Jesus began his 
ministry (around A.D. 28–29). According to Luke's account referenced in the supplementary material, his birth was itself remarkable: his aged parents, the priest 
Zechariah and Elizabeth, were told by the angel Gabriel they would have a son, and Zechariah was struck mute for his unbelief until the child was born. Elizabeth was 
a relative of Mary, making John and Jesus kinsmen. Gabriel named John before his birth—the name being a form of "Jehovah hath had mercy."

 **His Appearance and Manner of Life**

The primary text presents John as a striking desert figure. He preached "in the wilderness of Judaea," wore "raiment of camel's hair, and a leathern girdle about his
loins," and lived on "locusts and wild honey" (Matthew 3:1, 4; also Mark 1:6). This dress deliberately echoed the prophet Elijah (2 Kings 1:8), and the supplementary
material notes that John understood his own ministry—like Elijah's—as one of reform and preparation, in keeping with the popular expectation that Elijah would return 
before the Messiah.

 **His Message and Baptism**

John's preaching centered on a single urgent theme: "Repent ye; for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matthew 3:2). He identified himself with Isaiah's 
prophecy—"The voice of one crying in the wilderness, Make ye ready the way of the Lord" (Matthew 3:3). Crowds from Jerusalem, Judaea, and the region around the 
Jordan came out to be baptized by him in the river, "confessing their sins."

He was fierce with the religious leaders, calling the Pharisees and Sadducees "offspring of vipers" and warning them not to rely on their descent from Abraham, "for
I say unto you, that God is able of these stones to raise up children unto Abraham" (Matthew 3:7–9). His images were vivid and severe: the axe laid at the root of 
the trees, the winnowing fan clearing the threshing-floor, chaff burned "with unquenchable fire."

Crucially, John pointed beyond himself: "he that cometh after me is mightier than I, whose shoes I am not worthy to bear: he shall baptize you in the Holy Spirit and
in fire" (Matthew 3:11). This self-subordination is central to how the tradition remembers him.

 **The Baptism of Jesus**

When Jesus came from Galilee to be baptized, John hesitated—"I have need to be baptized of thee, and comest thou to me?"—but yielded when Jesus said it was fitting 
"to fulfil all righteousness." At the baptism, "the heavens were opened," the Spirit of God descended "as a dove," and a voice declared, "This is my beloved Son, 
in whom I am well pleased" (Matthew 3:14–17).

 **His Death**

Mark 6 gives the account of John's execution. John had rebuked Herod Antipas—"It is not lawful for thee to have thy brother's wife"—for marrying Herodias, his 
brother Philip's wife. Herodias wanted John killed but could not act, for Herod "feared John, knowing that he was a righteous and holy man," kept him safe, and 
"heard him gladly" despite being perplexed. The opportunity came at Herod's birthday feast, when the daughter of Herodias danced and pleased the king, who rashly 
swore to give her whatever she asked, up to half his kingdom. Prompted by her mother, she demanded "the head of John the Baptist" on a platter. The king was 
"exceeding sorry," but for the sake of his oath and his guests he had John beheaded in prison and his head brought on a platter.

The historian Josephus offers an independent report (from the *Antiquities of the Jews*), remembering John as "a good man" who urged the Jews to virtue and 
righteousness, and explaining Herod's motive politically: he feared John's great influence over the crowds might spark rebellion, and killed him to prevent trouble.

 **Themes and Significance**

Several threads run through the material:

- **Forerunner and witness.** John's role is consistently that of herald and witness. The Gospel of John especially emphasizes his testimony—one commentator notes 
that the Greek word for testimony, *marturia*, gives us "martyr." The Fourth Gospel even has John reduce himself: "He must increase, as I must decrease."

- **The Elijah pattern.** His clothing, wilderness setting, and reforming mission all evoke Elijah, connecting him to prophetic expectation.

- **Preparation for the Messiah.** John gathered a genuine following, but the tradition stresses that he directed that following toward Jesus.

- **A confusion after death.** Herod's guilty conscience surfaces in Mark 6, where he hears of Jesus' miracles and concludes, "John, whom I beheaded, he is 
risen"—showing the powerful impression John left even after his execution.
